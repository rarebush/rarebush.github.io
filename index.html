Overview

The 3 steps to creating a HUB

  1. Identity Specify and implement an identity

    The HUB identity will manifest itself in a number of ways. This includes everything from logos, to colours and the very content that appears on each page within the HUB. Although we can take the initiative on these aspects, deciding on these elements early in the HUB building process will give the best context for moving on with the later stages.

  2. Primary Content Pick and populate the HUB with content

    Almost all content surfaced within the HUB will be dynamically pulled from our database, which means you will not need to separately manage the content that appears within Wiley Online Library and your HUB.

  3. Supporting Content Apply supplementary content

    Throughout the HUB there are locations dedicated to serving extra pockets of content to the user called widgets. Widgets can vary from upcoming events to live twitter feeds.

Hub flow

How the HUB is structured

One purpose of the Hub is to help users find the content they wish to consume. This can be accomplished through search, a shared link or simply browsing through desired topics. The Hub structure is designed to aid the many ways users access your content.

The adjacent diagram illustrates the hierarchy of the Hub. The Hub home provides access to journals, which in turn lists issues, which then contain lists of articles. Accessing these articles then take the user to Anywhere Article, our article viewing platform where elements of your Hub will persist.

Identity Overview

How can we make your Hub reflect your brand?

In the following section we will present the various Hub pages mentioned before and articulate how and where we can accommodate your unique brand requirements.

This can include anything from logos and colours, to journal covers and key graphics or imagery.

However, it is important to be aware that a well thought out identity does not stop at the logos and colours shown on a website. It persists through the style of writing, the name and URL of the site, the style of photography, and many other aspects.

Some aspects of your Hub you may want to consider early in the process include:

Site URL domain name / web address

All Hubs published by Wiley follow the same domain name format: hubname.onlinelibrary.wiley.com

Color scheme

A Hub colour pallette should be restricted to two primary colours, a ‘brand’ color and an ‘action’ color. This can be extended to a third if necessary.

The brand color will be used for text links and detailing.

The action color will be used for important links, buttons, and other areas that require the user’s attention. It should be distinguishable from the brand color and will be used sparingly throughout the interface.

Colors can be supplied directly as either RGB values or HEX values. If this is not possible or you do not feel comfortable specifying these values, we can extract a color scheme from a logo or image. These 2 colors will be assisted by a range of grey tones (already specified) to aid with visual hierarchy, detailing and typography.

You can use this tool to see how your colors comply with web accessibility standards: http://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/

Imagery

Most of the imagery throughout the Hub will be directly connected to journals, issues and articles. However, there may be opportunities to showcase important events, featured issues or highlighted articles. In these situations, using an interesting, brand related image can reinforce the Hub identity and encourage more views.

Logos

There may be multiple logos that you wish to include on your Hub. Such as society logos, journal logos, affiliates, etc. A hub can accommodate all of these and designers at Wiley can help you produce the correct assets for web use.

Identity

Hub Home

Most of the elements and areas we describe here will persist throughout the entire Hub. We usually refer to these elements as ‘global elements’. A global element can be a persistent login button or the Hub logo, it simply remains in the same place on all pages making them great candidates for branding.

Throughout the Identity sections of this guide, we will slowly introduce all of the elements that can be 'branded' to make your hub unique. With each page in the Hub Flow several more elements will be introduced.

  • Header logo
  • Text links
  • Button links
  • Decoration
  • Footer logo

Header logo

The Hub logo will help users immediately identify the site they are on and also function as a quick link back to the homepage.

It reinforces and creates awareness for the Hub brand.

The logo should be neither ‘too horizontal’ or ‘too vertical’. A good ratio is the same as a landscape piece of A4 paper or 2:3.

INSERT RATIO IMAGE

Logos should function on a white background for best result, but we can accommodate logos that must have a particular background behind them.

Text links

Text links include the primary links in the header, as well as article titles and links to off-site materials. There are many more examples, but they should all adopt the same ‘brand’ color specified to give the user a consistent experience.

Button links

Like text links, buttons come in different shapes and sizes, often incorporating iconography. The button color will be taken from the ‘action’ color specified and will be used to draw the user’s attention and break up the design.

Decoration

There are a limited amount of decorative elements in a Hub. Typically reserved for signposting or dividing up content. A major example of this is the colour band that sits behind the search box in the header.

Decorations will typically use the brand color, but can sometimes use the action color.

Footer logo

Similar to how the header logo takes ownership over the page, the footer logo reinforces this.

The footer logo can be the very same that is used in the header. However, it is possible to specify a separate logo.

One use case is if the Hub is a sister site to a society's home site. The Hub uses an adapted version of the society’s logo to avoid confusion with the society’s primary site. In the footer the society wish to showcase their default logo as a stamp of ownership.

Identity

Journal Home

In the previous section, Hub Home, we covered most of the global elements that will appear on pages throughout the Hub. For this section we will look into branding elements that are unique to pages pertaining to a specific journal.

Many 'global' elements have been designed for the Hub home page, but there are a some that will will be unique to journal pages:

  • Header logo
  • Text links
  • Button links
  • Decoration
  • Footer logo
  • Journal banner
  • Journal primary cover
  • Featured carousel images

Journal banner

The Journal banner can be as important, if not more important than the Hub identity. For this reason we dedicate an entire horizontal band to the Journal’s branding.

The band is formed by 2 sections; the primary journal banner and a smaller supporting graphic.

The primary section should include the journal’s name (abbreviation, full name, strapline, etc.) as well as any decorative elements that reinforce the journal’s identity. This asset will typically be extracted from the journal’s cover.

Please keep in mind that Hubs are based on a light theme with a white background (to ensure compatibility with as many graphics as possible). Due to this feature, all journal banners must function on a white background. Solid blocks of background color are strongly discouraged as they can compete with the Hub’s global color scheme.

Journal primary cover

Throughout the Hub, it is possible to create image links for the various journals within a Hub (usually on the Hub home). We can use an image of the most recent issue or the journal logo. However, it may be worth picking one cover that members strongly affiliate with that particular journal. It is worth giving thought to what represents a journal outside of the journal’s domain.

Featured carousel images

On the Journal home page there is capacity for a feature carousel. The carousel cycles through selected articles and surfaces the images attached to them. Selecting the articles with interesting or well produced imagery can enhance the overall quality of the Hub.

Identity

Issues

The pages listing issues for a particular journal share most of the assets set out on the Journal home page. They retain the journal banner and the journal navigation bar.

Most Hub elements would have already been designed at this point, but there are a some that will be unique to Journal issues pages:

  • Header logo
  • Text links
  • Button links
  • Decoration
  • Footer logo
  • Journal banner
  • Journal primary cover
  • Featured carousel images
  • Issue thumbnail images

Issue thumbnail images

An inclusion on the Issues page that can reflect the Hub or journal brand, is the list of issue cover thumbnails. The assets are simply small versions of the issue covers, but there is a case here for ensuring your Journal covers adopt a consistent style, preferably one that can be identified at smaller scales.

Identity

Issue Contents

Styling for Issue Contents pages will be taken from the predefined colors and use elements that exist on previous pages. There is little styling that can applied directly to this page without changing the visuals throughout the rest of the Hub.

Identity

Search Results

The results page uses a unique layout to accommodate the facet column and results list. Because functionality is key on this page, effect on style change is limited to color. The various elements have already been designed and tested to work effectively.

Identity

Anywhere Article

Anywhere article is an interface designed specifically to consume articles. To ensure a consistent experience for the user between the articles they read, it utilises its own visual design.

To retain the Hub and journal identities, we use the same header and footer for the rest of the Hub. The journal branding is also repositioned to facilitate a more engaging article reading experience.

Identity

Generic pages

The styling prescribed throughout the previous Hub pages will be used to generate any supplementary pages that form part of the Hub. This includes colors, headers, footers, typography, etc.

Primary Content Overview

What content will appear on which pages?

Once a theme for the Hub has been finalised, we then decide on the content that will populate the Hub’s various page types.

The following sections will use the same structure from the identity portion of this document. We will move from page to page and suggest the kind of content users will look for and expect.

It is important to note that most pages use a 2-column approach. The larger left column is where primary content resides. The more narrow, right column contains widgets and supporting content.

Primary Content

Hub Home

Similar to the ‘Identity: Hub Home’ section, we will cover the majority of global content here that subsequent pages will adopt.

Areas where content can be customized include:

  • header content
  • footer content
  • hero section
  • journal links
  • topics
  • other important links or information

Header

A primary function of the header is to provide an accessible interface to the rest of the website. It is an element the user can rely upon in order to navigate to another part of the Hub. It is common to include frequently accessed parts of the site, without providing so many that the navigation becomes incomprehensible.

If multiple links are required, we can group them into ‘drop-down’ menus that collate multiple links into one container accessed by a single tab.

The header also supports a ‘secondary links’ section on the far right that can be used to simply link to supporting information and off-site links.

We recommend

‘Journals’ or ’Publications’
We suggest a tab that activates a drop-down containing all significant publications. These sub-links will direct users to the corresponding publication homepage within the Hub.

‘Topics’
If available, we can implement a taxonomy into the Hub browsing model. Allowing users to immediately navigate to a selection of articles that pertain to a single topic or subject area.

We do not recommend

Using external links in the place of tabs
A header takes ownership of the Hub and it can appear regressive if users are encouraged leave it. Please use the secondary link area or footer if you wish to include links to other websites.

Footer Content

The footer can operate in a similar way to the header, but with the advantage that it follows content the user is usually interested in and does not interfere with their flow.

We can therefore incorporate links to external sites, links to supporting pages, or even information relating to the Hub.

Hero Section

On the Hub home we can show a featured image or article that links to an article or other part of the Hub.

We recommend

An interesting, relevant, high-quality image that links to a popular or featured article.

We do not recommend

A low quality image that has little in common with the Hub. Nothing too stock-image like.

Journal Links

In many cases, users will want to navigate to specific journals. We can provide stand-out links to each journal homepage and their corresponding current issue, alongside journal imagery and issue information.

The information displayed can be adapted to suit your needs, but we do have some recommendations.

We recommend

For each journal within the Hub, we suggest:

  • a cover image or journal logo (links to Journal Homepage)
  • title of journal (links to Journal Homepage)
  • link to current issue
  • current issue number/date
  • link to previous issues (links to Journal Issues)

We do not recommend

We strongly discourage linking to multiple issues from multiple journals. This can affect hierarchy and confuse the user.

Topics

If provided, we can implement a tabulated list of links to various elements from your taxonomy. Each link will direct users to a Search Results pre-populated with the appropriate facet selected.

We recommend

We suggest you pick a top level taxonomy with no more than 20-30 topics.

We do not recommend

Do not attempt to show too many links, or links that exist multiple levels deep within the taxonomy structure. Too many links that are too granular will hinder the user when finding the results they want.

Other important links or information

Although we suggest using the element structure listed above, you can have custom content. Such as links to featured articles and issues, or small features and updates about a particular journal or society.

We recommend

We suggest keeping any extra content concise and limited in word length. Homepages are traditionally used as a means to an end - a collection of links and information that will help the user find the content they need.

We do not recommend

Avoid large quantities of text or too many images in varying sizes. Try to keep the page composition easy to digest and follow.

Primary Content

Journal Home

Most of the content on Journal home pages is dictated by the activity of that specific journal. The control we present to you is the criteria from which that content is shown. More will be explained in the following sections.

Elements:

  • Ad space
  • Journal nav links
  • carousel
  • browse article streams
  • Articles list
Ad Space

The primary space for advertisement on a Hub. It occupies the entire width of the site and takes the format of a letterbox. We follow the Interactive Advertising Bureau guidelines to ensure consistency and best practice.

We recommend

Keep the amount of text low and messages brief. Images can say a thousand words providing it isn’t too ambiguous.

We do not recommend

We discourage large amounts of small text and repurposing adverts built for other dimensions. Spend some time thinking about how the landscape form can tell a short story.

Journal navigation links

Many of these links will be straightforward, but there could be some potential in linking to ‘special collections’ or ‘highlighted articles’ if you can provide these kinds of grouped articles.

We recommend

Links that have gone before include:

  • Home
  • Issues
  • Virtual Issues
  • Highlights/Special collections
  • About (this journal)
  • Submit an article (to this journal)

We do not recommend

There should be no more than 6-8 links depending on character length. Otherwise there could be a risk of some links not showing due to there not being enough room in the navigation bar.

Carousel

Popular on many sites, a carousel can show multiple pieces of information whilst using a low footprint. Subtle animated transitions between content ‘slides’ can draw the user’s eye.

We recommend

The bare minimum of text with a strong graphic for each slide. Use no more than 3 slides as slides later than that in the sequence will get almost zero traffic.

We do not recommend

4 or more slides with large amounts of content and too brief a pause.

Article streams

Due to the immediacy and modularity of the internet, it is possible to publish or preview articles without releasing entire issues. We can list articles according to any number of properties that you have assigned to your articles.

We recommend

No more than several feeds of articles organised by the following:

  • Recently published
  • Most accessed articles
  • Most cited articles
  • Accepted articles

We do not recommend

Avoid singling out particular subcategories of your content without being certain of potential benefits. This presents a bias and can often undermine the rest of the content within your journals.

Articles

Articles will be listed according to a decided upon criteria. However, for each article, only essential information and abstracts will be visible to the user.

  • Article type (according to given taxonomy)
  • Article title
  • Author list
  • Published/accepted date
  • Links to Abstract, Full Text, PDF and References
  • Other content pulled from article (keypoints, images, etc.)

We recommend

Enquire your user base for what type of content they might find most useful at the article browsing stage, alongside the abstract. For example, 'key points', 'summary graphics', etc.

We do not recommend

Long abstracts or verbose article titles. This can be difficult to control, but guidance for authors to prepare their work in a consistent format can make browsing articles an easier experience for the user.

Primary Content

Issues

The type of content that appears here will usually be seen as a means to an end. User’s will more than likely know which issue they are interested in and so a clear interface through which they can arrive at that issue is crucial.

Elements we can effect change on include:

  • Journal Issues navigation
  • Journal Issues list
Journal Issues Navigation

The left hand rail will contain a table of contents for all issues pertaining to a journal. This unit will adopt the naming convention used by that particular journal. Lengthy lists can be truncated by ‘show more’ actions.

We recommend

Listing issues from newest to oldest using whatever naming convention current in play by the journal. If there are dozens, or hundreds of issues, bucket the issues into groups where possible, for example, by year or volume.

We do not recommend

Avoid changing the naming convention or altering the way issues are bucketed from the printed publication.

Journal Issues List

To the right of the issue navigation we list all issue pertaining to the chosen ‘bucket’ (year, volume, etc.). Each issue is accompanied by a cover image, name, publication date and pages (if part of a larger volume). We can also single out issues that are free or open-access.

We recommend

Differentiating issue with distinct covers if possible.

We do not recommend

Avoid changing the naming convention or altering the way issues are bucketed from the printed publication.

Primary Content

Issue Contents

In many ways the Issue Contents is similar to the Journal Home, although instead of pulling content from all issues, we show all the articles in order of how they are presented in a particular issue. There is also capacity for showing an issue cover as well as toggling the level of detail in the list of articles.

As most of the content is dynamically populated using the contents of actual issues, there is little room for change here. Here a list of the elements and functions:

  • Cover
  • Issue Title and Information
  • Brief/Detailed switch
  • Articles list
Cover

Similar to what is found on the Journal Issues, the cover of an issue can be displayed alongside the issue contents.

Issue Title and Information

Here we use the same name and information presented on the Journal Issues page.

Brief/Detailed switch

A simple switch that toggles all extra content on and off for each issue ‘article’. Content that is toggled includes:

  • Key points
  • Images
Article List

In relation with the switch listed above, the article list contains all separated instances within an issue, including articles, issue information and introductions.

Primary Content

Search Results

The results page is dynamically populated with content corresponding to the user's search criteria. The only area where customisation can occur is within the filter options on the left by the way of your Taxonomy and a few other quantifiable parameters.

Filters

If you have used online shopping sites before, you should be familiar with how filters work. In the case of Hubs we have a few limiters to choose from.

Filter by:

  • Journal
  • Taxonomy
  • Date of publication

Primary Content

Anywhere Article

As mentioned before, Anywhere Article is an interface designed specifically to consume articles. Content is pulled and organised straight from the author’s submission, so affect on change is limited to the global elements and specified advertising positions.

Advertising

There are 2 locations for advertising; a central ‘leaderboard’ that sits above the article and below the global header, and a left aligned ‘skyscraper’.

Both can be utilised to advertise events, membership, or anything that can be rendered as a still image.

Primary Content

Generic Pages

A Hub’s primary function is to illustrate journals, issues and articles. However, it still functions like any other website and can host any number of pages with simple content. Types of content can include:

  • Text
  • Links
  • Images
  • Video
  • Audio

We recommend

Some pages we recommend:

  • About (the society)
  • Contact (the society)
  • Author resources
  • Paper submission
  • Events

Supporting Content

Widgets are self contained pockets of content or functionality that exist within the right-hand column on many of the Hub’s pages.

Their modular nature allow for easy addition or removal of widgets depending on their page context. For example, it may be relevant to show the most recent issue for a journal within the journal pages, but not on the Hub home page.

Likewise, it may be more fitting to display links to social platforms pertaining to the society on the Hub home and journals on the respective journal homes.

Some of the widgets we will cover include:

  • Publish with…
  • Membership
  • Upcoming events
  • Advertisement/external or house ads
  • Social media links
  • Current issue (dynamic)
  • Twitter feed
  • Many more
Publish with…

A widget that primary supports authors and directs them to paper submission resources.

Membership

Used to advertise and direct users to the application process for joining a society.

Upcoming Events

An image associated with an upcoming event accompanied by links to find out more about the event.

Advertisement

An image advertisement for membership, events, subscriptions, etc.

Social media links

A list of links pointing to various social media platforms a society or journal has a presence on; complete with relevant graphics.

Current issue

The current issue of a particular journal with accompanying cover image, information and link to issue.

Twitter feed

A list of dynamically updated tweets from a specified feed.
